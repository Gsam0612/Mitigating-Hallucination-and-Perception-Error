Abstract
Visual hallucinations are common in multimodal large language models that act as vision- language agents (VLAs) thus undermining their trustworthiness and safety. Four main types of hallucinations are covered in this proposal namely object existence, misidentification, attribute or count, and spatial relation errors. This research suggest the optimization mechanism based on the Group Relative Policy (GRPO) fine-tuning that is easy to combined with a perceptual component. This pipeline incorporates detector based prompts, multi-view consistency, a chain of thought reasoning process augmented with self-verification, and a hallucination based reward signal which makes the model produce outputs that are based on verifiable visual evidence. Through a state-of-the-art object detector and scene parser, the textual outputs of the agent are grounded in real images and in the process, conflicting statements are alleviated through the use of cross-view verification. GRPO is a simplified version of Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), which does not use a separate value network, instead giving group-based outcome benefits; this simplification preserves the fidelity but takes advantage of simulated ground-truth rewards. Both training and evaluation annotated datasets are created using a wide variety of interactive scenarios simulated in the Unity engine. Performance gains are measured in terms of hallucinating rate, object mention precision/recall rate, counting accuracy, spatial consistency and calibration fidelity.
  The literature review will comprise the history of vision- language models, classification of hallucinations, benchmark datasets, and the existing mitigation methods that are being employed at present. The project is expected to yield the following contributions: (1) multimodal truthfulness alignment by reinforcement-learning; (2) an open-source Unity benchmark on visual hallucinations; (3) a modular recipe to combine detection, multi-view reasoning, chain-of-thought, and RL fine-tuning; and (4) a professional, legal, ethical, and social (PLES) guide to safe deployment.

1.1 Background and Motivation
The use of large vision-language models (VLMs) and agents (VLAs) has completely trans- formed multimodal AI by allowing systems which can see and say, i.e. they view pictures or videos and can respond in natural language in real-time. Models such as LLaVas demon- strate good results on open-ended visual question answering and chatting through the combination of potent visual encoders and large language backbones[1]. Let you put them into some mobile robot or a simulated avatar, and you will have an embodied agent who can obey language instructions, tell you what it is looking at, and devise maneuvers around complex spaces.
  Despite all that development, however, VLAs continue to experience high hallucination rate: they will tend to mention objects that are not there, make wrong identifications, count wrongly, or represent spatial objects in a incorrect way [3, 7]. Hallucinations only reduce accuracy in tasks of static captioning, whereas in embodied environments they can directly cause unsafeness or cause the robot to malfunction. An example is when an agent may say that there is no one around a hallway when it is not, overlooking a hazard, or even creating a tool that it believes it requires to perform a task. Multimodal system benchmarks including hallucination and safety recently show that these perception failures are the primary barrier to reliable deployment. [3, 7, 13]
  The problem is aggravated in the case of interactive or multi-view. As the number of objects increases, there is an increase in the hallucinations since the model places a lot of weight on the priors in the datsets and the language statistics rather than concrete visual evidence [1, 3]. Standards such as MIHBench demonstrate that hallucinations may occur or change depending on the camera view when you do not impose cross-view consistency [3]. Safety-oriented embodied benchmarks display that misperceptions cut into risky courses of action [7, 13].
  This is why this project aims at aligning VLAs with visual truth when there is training in embodied environments. Instead of merely adjusting prompts or providing post-hoc filters

2 Literature Review

2.1 Vision-Language Models and Embodied Agents.:
Today LVLMs are much more powerful and use the visual encoders in addition to high- capacity language models. Systems such as LLaVA also learn to refine an image encoder, which is a ViT-like model, on pairs of instructions and model outputs and also learn on conversation data. That allows the model to perform open-ended image-based visual question answering, captioning and multi-turn dialogue [5]. What you have is a general
-purpose tool, ideal at least in the case of embodied agents, be it simulation or real world, where the model must not merely be able to describe what it is seeing, but what to do next. Meanwhile, the object recognition remains a central part of embodied perception. One- stage detectors such as YOLOv8 (released in 2023) have been improved through multiple generations and today provide you with a real-time detector and instance segmentation with improved accuracy as compared to previous models[2]. Most embodied systems input the output of the detector, which is bounding boxes, classes, and a small number of attributes, as a compressed scene description to an LVLM policy, rather than actual pixels. That assists in grounding and reduces the number of hallucinations which are purely
language driven.
  LVLMs are now as tested in more complex situations. An example is the introduction of a multimodal interactive safety benchmark by IS -Bench. It verifies the performance in VLM-driven agents in carrying out home tasks in the safety conditions and proper se- quencing of mitigating risk measures [7]. SafeAgentBench also works in a similar manner, but concerns planning tasks with safety-conscious agents, demonstrating that a significant number of systems will accept potentially dangerous instructions with a very small proba- bility of refusing [13]. These works underscore the fact that despite effective instructions after and visual recognition, the safety and grounded behavior do not depend solely on the presence of a model, which is inclined towards language biases or misinterprets visual signals.

  This project is based on that direction, in which LVLMs are viewed as not only perception- reasoning engines, but also policies the hallucination of which can be trained using a reinforcement learning at training time. Unity based simulator and GRPO training loop is an attempt to combine the capabilities of the modern detectors [2] and LVLMs [5, 9] with an explicit goal of minimizing the number of hallucinations.

2.2 Taxonomy of Hallucinations
Hallucination simply refers to the model producing something with confidence without being supported by the inputs. That is nasty in LVLMs since the model may sound per- suasively visual even at times the model is incorrect. Recent efforts have shifted to more definite taxonomies. Chen et al. introduced the term multi-object hallucination to refer to a multi-object query being invented by an LVLM, or confounded by the co-occurring items, or incorrectly counting an item [1]. They discovered that hallucinations increase with the dispersion of the attention of the model on a number of objects- and it is a fundamental weakness of multi-object reasoning.
In embodied settings, this research focus on four important types of hallucinations:
Object existence hallucination - saying an object exists when it does not.
Object misidentification hallucination - misclassifying an object.
Attribute hallucination- hallucinating properties such as colour, quantity or condition. Spatial-relation hallucination-This is a misrepresentation of the location of an object with regard to another object.
  Zhu et al [15]. demonstrated that LVLMs are capable of building a spatial bias, and they match language templates to positional tokens in the training information. That results in systematic errors in relational reasoning, that is precisely the type of spatial-relation hallucination are addressing here.
  Liu et al [6]. provide the first comprehensive survey of hallucinations in LVLMs, synthe- sising benchmarks, causes, and mitigation methods. They distinguish between perception- level hallucinations (errors in object, attribute or relation recognition), reasoning-level

hallucinations (logical inconsistencies or unsupported intermediate steps), and instruction- level hallucinations (misinterpretation or over-generalisation of user instructions). The given taxonomy is appropriate with the embodied agents, as perception errors may lead to unsafe physical behavior, and reasoning errors may lead to task constraints or safety violations.
  According to Xiao and Wang [12], to manage hallucination, uncertainty estimation is required. They demonstrated that models that estimate their self-uncertainty in captioning make more reliable predictions and allow downstream systems to understand when to trust predictions. In the case of a safety-critical embodied agent, the auditory feedback to the visual comprehension is equally important as the increase of the raw accuracy.
  In general, the literature indicates that hallucination in LVLMs is a complicated issue, and it needs a taxonomy that is fined to come up with effective solutions. I will apply the four aforementioned types as assessment criteria of both training-time RL and ablation experiment.

It demonstrates that the latest models to be even capable of responding confidently to trick prompts or implicit visual confounders, which contributes to the importance of further diagnostic grounding tests.

2.4 Reasons behind Hallucination in LVLMs
It is important to know the reasons why LVLMs hallucinate in order to design good mitigation. There are several patterns continuing to emerge in the books. To begin with, excessive dependence on language priors is a major motivation. LVLMs frequently make

Mitigating Hallucination and Perception Errors in Vision Language Agents  •  11

guesses about probable objects or features on the text, although these guesses are not justified in the image. Chen et al. demonstrate that the LVLMs impose hallucinations on objects that can frequently be present in the training data in multi-object settings (such as the creation of a fork next to a dining table) [1]. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompts in multimodal tasks, as proposed by Wang et al. and others, imply that reasoning prompts can not only be beneficial, as they can encourage more grounding, but can also be detrimental as they can generate more language priming when the model does not have a strong visual cue [14].
  Second, it is centered around dataset bias and weak visual grounding. Zhang et al. show that LVLMs have spatial biases, such that shared relative configurations (e.g. sky above ground) are leaked into the prediction even when images are manipulated to violate such configurations [15] Other causes of hallucination described in a survey conducted by Liu et al. include noisy or mismatched training captions, the under-representation of rare objects, and maladjusted fusion modules that under-represent visual tokens relative to text [6] [14]. Third, it is becoming increasingly apparent that even fusion and decoding mechanisms in themselves can be the cause of a hallucination. Wang et al. examine the interaction between the formulations of the instructions and the Q-Former architecture adopted in most LVLMs and demonstrate that some disturbances of the instructions such as the ones that state: you are a confused detector can systematically amplify hallucination by destabilizing multimodal alignment [10]. They suggest Instruction Contrastive Decoding (ICD), which involves comparing tokens distributions under both normal and disturbed instructions as well as penalizing tokens that tend to increase more when the model is confused (see inf.uni-hamburg.de). This demonstrates that hallucination is not just a training data or training weight problem: it can be affected (or reduced) also at inference, using decoding
tricks.
  Lastly, Mukherjee et al. believe in models being over-confident in the results of hallu- cination and state that uncertainty-aware training and calibration can aid in detecting

12  •  Vedhagiri Alagesan

low-trust predictions [12]. In safety-critical embodied tasks, this means that the hallucina- tion problem is two-fold, that is, decrease the frequency with which it occurs and increase the sensitivity of the agent to the possibility that it could still be false.
  In the context of this dissertation, such causes encourage the combination of a mitigation strategy which includes:
• More efficient visual grounding through explicit object detection and multi-view consistency.
• Hallucination-conscious reward signals which are punitive to language-prior-driven errors.
• Thoughtful decoding, CoT design, which might subsequently incorporate concepts such as ICD [10]

2.5 Inference-Time Mitigation: Decoding and Prompting.
Large portion of new research is focused on hallucination at inference time, without accessing model weights. These techniques are popular in the sense that they can be applied to closed source or API only models and are inexpensive to implement. One of such is Instruction Contrastive Decoding (ICD) [10]. By introducing a confounded instruction (e.g. prefixing with a role that promotes confusion) and contrastingly penalizing tokens more prone to receive this confounded instruction Wang et al. demonstrate that you can reduce object-level hallucination on POPE and many other benchmarks without reducing or decreasing F1 scores. Their findings demonstrate that decoding may be regarded as self-diagnostic, whereby the instability of the model in perturbed situations is used to indicate hallucination-prone tokens.
  Alternatives, like contrastive decoding LVLM versus a more conservative text-only model, or multiple LVLM checkpoints, are also based on the same principle: identify hallucination candidates as disagreements and suppress them during decoding. The survey by Liu et al. [6] further divides the inference-time mitigation into prompt engineering (such as adding explicit grounding instructions, think step by step and checking the image),

Mitigating Hallucination and Perception Errors in Vision Language Agents  •  13

reranking (selecting the least hallucinated candidate among a series of samples), and external verification (checking generated statements with the help of a tool or a detector). These tricks are inference time tricks, though they are very practical to implement and much of the time reactive. They do not modify the tendency of LVLM to hallucinate; they simply interfere or conceal poor results. On the side of an embodied-agent, that is restrictive: even hallucinations during intermediate reasoning stage (e.g., CoT justification
which is never displayed to the user but influences action choice) can destroy safety.
  Although this is that which these inference-time tricks do, they are easy to add, but remain fairly reactive. They do not alter the basic predilection of the LVLM to hallucinate; they merely intercept or conceal inopportune products. That is a genuine issue to an agent doing embodied agents due to the possibility that the hallucinated intermediate reasoning steps such as chain-of-thought justifications which do not reach the user but cause the system to act can still make it unsafe.
  The project thus considers such approaches as a side effect of a greater repair and will apply reinforcement learning to condition the decision-making process of the LVLM in such a way that it gets less grounded and less prone to hallucinating even within the chain-of-thought.

2.6 Training-Time Mitigation and GRPO-Style reinforcement learning.
Training-time mitigation aims to modify the model rather than only the decoding of the model. According to a survey by Liu et al. there are a few methods of doing so: fine-tuning on counter-hallucination datasets, preference-based alignment (such as DPO prompts to incentivize grounded outputs), and RL with hallucination-sensitive rewards[6]. The bulk of this remains preliminary and there is very little in the way of exploring embodied, simulation-based RL of LVLMs.
  The recent articles on the Group Relative Policy Optimisation (GRPO) and PPO-type approaches provide us with a strong theoretical and practical foundation to train large policy with sparse or noisy feedback [8, 11, 13]. Shi's tutorial on vision researchers is

14  •  Vedhagiri Alagesan

a framework that views PPO/GRPO as a means of matching models to complex visual objectives such as consistency across views, safety constraints [13].. The Fast-Slow Thinking GRPO by Xiao and Gan recommends the training of agents with quick reactive and slow deliberative functions and aligning them together by policy optimisation [11]. That concept is similar to the project scheme of combining rapid perception (an object detector, fast thinking) with slower self-verification processes.
  Within the LVLM space in particular, Chen et al. [1], Li et al. [3] and others are tackling hallucination as a reward-design problem: award a bonus when the predictions match the ground-truth objects, attributes, relations etc. and penalize when the model is hallucinating. However, the majority of tests are offline analysis, unlike closed-loop RL training.
Our framework adds to this by:
• Constructing a Unity-based simulation over which one can construct ground-truth annotations (object lists, attributes, positions) and align them with predictions made by LVLM at each timestep.
• The hallucination reward functions should be defined to punish four forms of hallucination (existence, misidentification, attribute, spatial) yet the task-level recall should remain.
• Running a GRPO-type RL cycle to make small improvements to the LVLM pol- icy, effectively implementing prompt-based mitigation measures (e.g. CoT, self- verification) into the internal behaviour of the model rather than simply moving them up the inference pipe.

They also do not consider hallucination and safety as correlated measures (such as the success rate vs. the rejection rate) but rather connect hallucination to a learning reward.

2.8 Synthesis and Recommendations for Further Action
The literature reviewed above leads to several key insights:
  The hallucination is all over and in a variety of forms: LVLMs have illusions of objects, attributes, relations and even chains of reasoning particularly in multi object, multi view scenes [1, 3, 6, 15]. The simple increase of model size or pre-training data does not help.
  Existing benchmarks are present but fragmented: POPE, MOH and MIHBench pro- vide good diagnostic indicators whereas ISBench and SafeAgentBench are concerned with safety [7, 8, 13]. However, no one has an integrated environment that judges hallucination,

task achievement and safety simultaneously in an embodied environment with learning feedback.
  Inference tricks are useful but do not change the model weights: Methods such as Instruction Contrastive Decoding [10] and grounded prompt engineering are capable of reducing hallucinations without retraining, but do not alter the inner workings of the model. They also find it difficult to deal with hallucinations that occur in concealed intermediate logical processes.
  LVLMs used in the context of an embodied environment are underutilized using training-time RL and preference-based methods: Although theoretically, GRPO and analogous algorithms are well-known, very little has been done to apply them to vision- language agents, using hallucination-aware rewards, even in high-fidelity simulators [8, 11, 13].
On the basis of those gaps, this research recommends and adopts the following structure:
• Construct a Unity benchmark which resembles MIHBench and IS-Bench, providing controllable scenes, multi-view views and ground-truth annotations to the existence of all objects, their attributes and spatial relationships [3, 7].
• Develop clear hallucination measures of the four types of hallucinations and treat them both as the assessment criteria and part of the RL reward.
• Integrate modular perception and reasoning: apply a YOLOv8 style detector to structured scene representations [2], an LVLM, say LLaVA, to reason and generate language and self-verify with inspired ICD-style contrastive reasoning [10].
• Use a GRPO-type RL loop to optimize the LVLM to the hallucination-sensitive rewards, both terminal answers and intermediate chain-of-thought [6, 8, 11].

4.2 Unity Simulation and Dataset Generation
To systematically test our approach, designed three categories of Unity scenes:

(1) Simple rooms contain just a few objects with clear visibility. These serve as our initial calibration environments where the model should perform well.
(2) Cluttered rooms feature many overlapping objects that frequently trigger multi- object and spatial hallucinations [1, 3, 14].
(3) Hazard scenarios are inspired by benchmarks like IS-Bench and SafeAgentBench [7, 13]. These environments require the agent to accurately perceive dangerous items such as knives, hot surfaces, and fragile objects to behave safely.

  Every frame comes with comprehensive ground-truth annotations including object lists, attributes, and pairwise spatial relationships. Split these scenarios into training, validation, and test sets to support both reinforcement learning and rigorous final evaluation.

4.3 Baseline VLA Pipeline
Before implementing our mitigation strategies, establish a baseline to measure the extent of the hallucination problem. The baseline agent follows a straightforward process:

(1) Capture one or more RGB frames from the Unity environment.
(2) Feed the primary frame to the vision-language model with a simple prompt that includes no grounding information or chain-of-thought structure.
(3) Record the model's generated caption or answer.
(4) Calculate hallucination metrics by comparing against ground truth, looking for incorrect objects, missing objects, extra objects, and mismatches in attributes and spatial relations [1, 4, 15].

  This baseline gives us concrete numbers on how often and in what ways the model hallucinates without intervention, providing reference points for measuring improvement.

4.4 Modular Mitigation Components
4.4.1 Object-Detection Grounding. integrating a YOLO-style object detector [2] that pro- cesses the same frames as the vision-language model. The detector produces a structured list of objects with confidence scores, which inject directly into the VLM prompt as a "scene summary." For example:
Detected objects: {cup, plate, microwave, countertop}. Based on this list and the image, answer the question...
  This grounding strategy explicitly discourages the model from mentioning objects not present in the detector's output. During training, the reward function penalizes any claims about objects that lack detection support.
4.4.2 Multi-View Consistency. Drawing inspiration from MIHBench [3], capture multiple camera views of the same scene-for instance, front and side perspectives. The agent can process these views in two ways:
• Concatenated processing: Combine visual and textual information from all views into a single input.
• Separate processing with consistency checking: Generate answers indepen- dently for each view, then run a consistency checker to flag contradictions.
  If the model gives different answers for different views, either give it negative rewards or ask it more self-verification questions. This pushes the agent to make sense of conflicting evidence and come to a clear picture of what is going on.
4.4.3 Chain-of-Thought and Self-Verification. Adopt chain-of-thought prompting strategies from recent multimodal reasoning work [11, 14]. Rather than jumping straight to an answer, the agent is prompted to:
(1) Enumerate visible objects and their attributes.
(2) Reason explicitly about spatial relationships.
(3) Synthesize this information into a final, concise answer.

Mitigating Hallucination and Perception Errors in Vision Language Agents  •  23

  This research also incorporate self-verification queries-questions like "Are you sure there is a knife? Re-check the image and detected objects." These prompts give the model a chance to catch and correct its own hallucinations before committing to a final response. This approach is inspired by work on uncertainty-aware captioning and behavioral consistency [12].


4.5 GRPO Training Procedure
Using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [8, 11] to fine-tune the vision-language model with hallucination-aware rewards. The training loop works as follows:

(1) Sampling: For each scene, the current policy generates multiple candidate outputs, including both chain-of-thought reasoning and final answers.
(2) Scoring: Each candidate receives a reward score based on:
(a) Negative penalties for hallucinated objects, incorrect attributes, or wrong spatial relations (verified against ground truth).
(b) Positive rewards for correctly mentioning objects, accurate counting, and safe recommendations.
(c) Minor penalties for excessive verbosity or off-topic content to prevent overly long chain-of-thought explanations.
(3) Grouping: Candidates are organized into small groups, and compute relative ad- vantages within each group to identify better versus worse outputs.
(4) Policy Update: update the VLM parameters using GRPO, shifting probability mass toward outputs that are more grounded and safer. Importantly, this approach doesn't require training a separate critic network.

  Throughout training, This research monitor performance on validation scenes and use early stopping plus hyperparameter tuning guided by hallucination and task-level metrics.

4.6 Evaluation Protocol
The evaluation framework addresses both hallucination reduction and practical task per- formance:
(1) Hallucination Metrics: Measure object-existence errors, misidentification rates, attribute error rates, and spatial relation accuracy, following benchmarks like Hallu- cinBench [1, 4, 15].
(2) Multi-View Consistency: Track how often the model contradicts itself across different camera views [3].
(3) Uncertainty and Calibration: Qualitatively assess whether the model appropri- ately expresses uncertainty (e.g., "I'm not sure") rather than confidently stating incorrect information [12].
(4) Task Success and Safety: Drawing from IS-Bench and SafeAgentBench [7, 13], measure success rates on navigation and manipulation tasks, as well as the frequency of hazard-related mistakes.
And conducting ablation studies by comparing:
(1) Baseline VLA versus various combinations of mitigation modules (detection only, detection + multi-view, detection + CoT, full system).
(2) GRPO-trained models versus models without reinforcement learning.
  These comparisons help us understand which components contribute most to reducing hallucinations and improving safe behavior.

Developers of embodied VLAs must take professional obligations regarding designing the system with the following requirements:
• Rigorous testing under various conditions, including adversarial and edge cases
• clear documentation of limitations of residual hallucinations and the recommended operating conditions
• Fail-safe system design with regard to emergency stop handling and cautious default strategies when under conditions of high uncertainty [7, 9, 13].

5.2 Legal and Regulatory Frameworks
As the embodied AI system transitions from the research environment to the marketplace, it must conform to new AI regulatory and product safety laws. Some of the legal aspects that come into play include the following:
• Liability:If the agent causes damage based on hallucinated perceptions, the liability could lie with hardware suppliers, software developers, and system integrators collectively.
• Compliance:The risk-based regulation of AI may consider embodied assistants as being of high-risk and may demand visible risk management [3, 13].
• Data Protection:The collection and processing of visual information within the context of the home environment could pose certain issues of privacy.
  Through the loggings of the perception and reasoning traces, the proposed system facilitates pre-deployment testing and post-incident investigations.

Note that the ethical issues lie with issues of equity, explainability, and user trust. Given the possibility of different hallucination rates between various settings (cluttered vs. uncluttered settings), certain users may be more prone to failures or dangers [3, 13, 15]. A solution to this problem must thus assess different settings.
  There's a need for transparency too. Clients tend to overestimate the results of fluent- language; thus, there should be clear communication of guesses via phrases like 'Not sure' or 'Can't see the object' because of the study on uncertainty by the researchers [12]. 5.5 Conclusion and Limitations
This proposal has discussed an requirement-driven research study on the mitigation of hallucinations in Vision-Language embodied agents on a Unity simulation platform via GRPO reinforcement learning. A discussion of the literature showed that the problem of multi-modal hallucinations is ubiquitous, structurally modeled, and highly hazardous when considering embodied scenarios [1, 3, 7, 13, 15]. • Detector-grounded prompts through YOLO-style perception [2].
• Multi-view consistency checks based on the MIHBench approach [3].
• Chain of thought reasoning/self-verification [14].
• GRPO-based training with a focus on multimodal alignment [8, 11].
  Requirements analysis and methodology illustrate how abstract system goals are realized as system design. The discussion on PLES places the technical endeavor into the context of more general discussions on AI safety, accountability, and its effects on society [7, 9, 12, 13]

• Initial reliance on simulation, which may not accurately relate to reality.
• Dependence on detector quality and area of expertise.
• Sensitivity of RL algorithms to reward definition and hyperparameters that might result in too cautious behavior.
  Future works may include the application of the proposed approach on real robots with the incorporation of advanced VLM detectors, and other calibration methods of verbalized confidence [9, 15].
